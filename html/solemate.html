SOLEMATE

Step Into Rehabilitation

April 2019 - June 2019

Role - UX/UI Designer

Teammate - Kristine Khieu, Surabhi Kalyan, Mona Luo, Steven Tran, Patrick Zhang

Overview

Solemate solves the unmet clinical need of hospital readmission and rehabilitation through a smart shoe insole and software system. Solemate manufactures a low-profile, low-power shoe insole equipped with strategically placed force resistive sensors. For the first time, patients will be able to track their progress day by day, and in turn, physicians will be able to better personalize treatment.

In the Spring of 2019 a colleague asked if I was interested in temporarily helping his start up, Solemate. The core issue was that their designer specialized in graphic design, and they quickly found through user tests that their app design was flawed. In the next three months I worked alongside a team of developers and their designer to improve their app.

DESIGN PROCESS

Onboarding

Previous Research

The first thing that I did was to have the founders and their designer catch me up with the progress they’ve made and what relevant information I should know such as their customer research and ideation.

The Problem

Patients with a lower extremity injury spend most of their rehabilitation at home rather than at the clinic. Typically the doctor will recommend putting X amount of pressure on the injured foot, but it is extremely difficult for patients to accurately gauge how much pressure they are putting on their foot. This most likely leads to re-injury as clinics provide little feedback for patients after the initial visit.

The Solution

The solution comes in two parts: A mobile app and a smart shoe insole. The mobile app is meant to provide a way for patients to test their foot pressure and see just how much weight they are putting on a foot in real time. The app then summarizes the data and provides patients with an easy to digest chart tracking their progress. Doctors are then able to provide advice based off of these results.

Smart Insole with three pressure sensors

Current App

I worked closely with Mona Luo, their graphic designer, to discuss her design process for their current hi-fi prototype and what sort of research they had done. What I learned was that Solemate needed hi-fi prototypes for their pitch slides so these screens were whipped up without much research, mostly on what the founders felt was appropriate and Mona’s own intuition.

App Critique

I spent my first meeting with Solemate workshopping their current design with the team and giving some critiques to begin paving the way forward.

Other Issues

Visual Design

 The colors were a bit harsh on some pages and didn’t have much reasoning for why those specific colors were chosen. Visual hierarchy and focus states can be improved as well as updating the overall aesthetic to feel more friendly rather than a cold, medical app.


User Flows

 A lot of the user flow felt redundant, such as on boarding that happens each time someone tries to start a test. A lot of the on boarding pages are also irrelevant such as ‘Make sure you’re connected to bluetooth’ which could be an error caught before even starting the test.

Moving Forward

Mona informed me that the design process was pretty rushed since they just had to get something out for a pitch conference at UCSD, however, since we had no impending deadlines I thought it best to go through the design process again in full and try to catch simple mistakes early on and focus on the ‘why’ for all design decisions.

Empathize

Understand Users

Since Kristine worked as a PT aid at Rady Children's Hospital we were lucky enough to be able to interview real patients and doctors/therapists. I wanted to collect some more data while interviewing as a designer rather than a founder.

Over the course of two weeks we were able to chat with two physical therapists and four patients. Our main goal with these interviews were to:

Determine what information is useful for a doctor and if these differ from a patient's.

Determine what patients value in their rehabilitation experience.

Gauge interest in both doctors and patients for the app itself.

What are common issues that occur during a rehabilitation process.

Consolidating Interview Notes

Key Points

Multiple Rehab Sessions.

 Rehabilitation can take months and this is mostly because each 'session' will last around four to six weeks where each session will ask for a different target weight. These target weights are usually standardardized so users should be able to do this.


Data should be simple to understand and easy to access.

 The only information both doctors and patients need to know is how well the patient has been applying the target weight. Users need to be presented this information so that they can easily understand it and it should most likely be on the home page.


Reinjury is the most dangerous and common issue.

 The healthcare industry spends approximately $12 B per year due failed rehabilitation. The most common issue being reinjury of bone/muscle or equipment malfunctions.


Interest is higher in doctors than in patients.

 We found that therapists are much more interested in having this sort of technology than patients would be. Kristine and Surabhi already anticipated this which is why app use would be like a prescription from their doctors. That being said, having a well designed app is still important for user satisfaction.

Problem Statement

Although Solemate had already identified the problem they were setting out to tackle, I thought it would be best to reframe it into a statement that we can constantly refer back to for design guidance.

How might we allow users to accurately go through rehabilitation sessions by themselves while also recording their progress in a way that both doctors and patients can easily digest and act upon?

Feature Prioritization

A lot of the existing features they had for the initial app design were pretty solid, but just needed to be improved upon. I took some time with Mona to go over what features we thought were relevant, which ones needed to be improved, and what new ideas we have based off of the research we had done.

Notes on Features

Since our developers were going to be leaving for the summer, we sat down with our backend and frontend developer to assess which features would be feasible in the time that we had, and which ones should be left for a later time. We did this by creating a MoSCoW diagram. The features we decided to approach are highlighted dark.

Prototype &
Testing

Wireframes

Based off of the original design and the work we had done prior, we began creating wireframes for the app. Our main focus was ensuring that each feature we designed would still be consistent with our problem statement.

Core Screens

User Testing 1

Mona and I tested our wireframes on eight users. Four have had injuries before while the other four didn't. We felt that this would ensure that our data isnt skewed because of some bias.

We found several issues with our design immediately. For example, the home page was too overwhelming for users. There weren't any groupings so the information was simply too much.

We also saw that our bottom nav had some issues. Specifically, users were confused about the test button. Our original idea was that offsetting the button from the rest would make it stand out, but the lack of a lable proved to be inconsistent and confusing for users.

 Our sign up process also had some issues where users didn't know the information we were asking for and would be confused about how to proceed.

Visual Design

We decided to go with softer, more pastel colors to make it easier on the eyes. We focused on having soft blues and teal greens to emphasize a sense of calmness and healing.

We also redid their logo since their previous logo felt very old-school and cold. We wanted something a bit more playful and modern as it reflects Solemate’s ambitions better.

User Testing 2

Once we had converted the wireframes into hi-fi prototypes, we ran another set of user tests under similar conditions hoping that we accounted for all the issues in the first round of tests.

We saw a huge improvement from the first round of testing, and decided to run a heuristic evaluation ourselves just to validate our results further. The one last feature we found based off of qualitative data was that after completing a test, having some sort of tip or affirmation would improve the experience.

Helpful tip after test

Final Design

Sign up pages have improved visual hierarchy and ability to input multiple rehab sessions.

Requested information is only those that directly affect rehabilitation.

Information is a lot more organized and usable. Key components are grouped in cards for visibility.

Removed a lot of superfluous content that didn’t serve any important purpose.

Improved onboarding is cut from four pages to two. Only provides useful information and is also skippable.

Live test shows numerical and visual data. Timer is added in at the top.

Reflection

This was one of the first projects that I was taking lead on in terms of design so the overall experience was very educational. I think working with Mona was a fantastic experience because we were constantly teaching each other new things. I was more design process focused, while Mona was able to teach me some things about graphic design and illustration. I think having to work with not only the team, but also coordinating with healthcare professionals made the process a lot more serious and the work that we were doing seemed to have immediate effects on people.

I think working with such a capable team has enforced a greater appreciation for open dialogue during any stage of the design process. Although the founders of Solemate were very set on what they wanted, it was better that I could bring in more design principles to inform our decisions. As a designer we must be careful listeners, but also an active expresser.
