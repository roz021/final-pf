Overview

Context

Once we had an initial prototype for our desktop app, I was tasked with redesigning their website into something the founders felt comfortable displaying to investors and at conventions. Since both founders were off on summer internships we gave ourselves most of the summer to craft our website.


Problem

Once I completed the initial designs for our POC, I was tasked with designing a company website for Exalton. Up until this point, they only had a minimal website detailing a bit about the company and who the CEO and CTO were. Now that we almost had a POC completed, we also needed a website that we could use to build an online presence and market our product.


Solution

I spent the next two months researching and designing the Exalton website. By expanding further on the brand the Exalton website also became a huge milestone in understanding the look and feel of Exalton as a whole. By using custom, isometric illustrations and an overall modern aesthetic, we hoped to show that we can compete with other well-known companies.

Design Process

Empathize

Research

The first step I took before designing a single page was to do some research on web design, specifically for business purposes, and from there, how to make the website stand out among the sea of other start ups. I then followed this up with some user research. I planned a couple interviews with a few of my colleagues at UCSD. The main goal for me was to understand how users navigate a company’s website as well as what they are looking for, especially with start ups that don’t have a reputation yet. On top of this, I began browsing other company websites to get a feel for the trends especially with other start ups.

Key Findings

Users need to easily understand what we do and how we do it.

However, the 'how you do it' should be easy to understand and remain high-level

CTA is crucial for start-ups, but too many will seem annoying for users.

Allow users to easily contact us.

Incorporate animations in a meaningful way to enhance interaction and stand out.

Define

The Problem

Once I felt comfortable with the research I had done, I created a problem statement that encapsulates the ‘why’ for the whole website design by using a ‘How Might We…’ statement.

How can we easily explain what Exalton is and push visitors to want to learn more?

Project Objectives

Create a polished company website.

As with any other company, their website is crucial for their brand and identity. It is most likely what their potential customers will see first so information has to be presented in a clear and logical way. The site also needs to look nice and have something that is constantly keeping the user’s attention.


Consolidate all useful information in one place.

There’s always a lot of explaining that happens when people are interested in a new company, especially if they use technology that is beyond the common scope. This means that all relevant information that any potential customer way want should be provided, and if not, an easy way to contact us should be available.


Continue building the brand.

As mentioned before, the website is crucial for a company’s brand. Therefore, the website should encapsulate the company’s energy be it in visual design, interaction design, or content.


Increase Business

The site should be like a flagship store for the company. User’s should be able to sign up for an account or a newsletter to be updated on company developments. Having some sort of connection through a newsletter would increase customer retention, or in our case, customer conversion.

Personas

Based of our objectives, we created two types of personas who would primarily visit the website. This helped me prioritize functionality and how information should be presented.

Ideate

Architecture

The architecture we decided to go with followed a flat navigation. We wanted to keep things simple with only four main tabs a user could visit. The landing page would capture a bit of everything while also providing links to enter into every other page. This was so that the information presented was very fluid, almost like a narrative. We knew that most people prefer scanning over reading, so information was also presented in a way that promotes that without losing core details.

Feature Prioritization

I began thinking of specific features for the website and how the architecture would be laid out. These sections were then further defined with the rest of the team before being handed off to the executive members for content creation.

Notes on features

Moodboard

I also created a moodboard to help give me a better sense of direction for the website’s style as well as provide the rest of the team an idea as to how the website would look. We wanted our website to feel more personal than others so that when users visited the site, it would feel a lot like one of us was actually speaking to them. In order to accomplish this we wanted to have playful illustrations and a friendly tone throughout all content.

User Testing

After the conference I had time to revise the site. Since I already had the live site I began testing with that. We ran into two big issues with our site: content and layout. On both the landing and product page our copy was not as user friendly as we had thought. Too much technical jargon littered the feature highlights on the product page, and text within the landing page which is supposed to be a very friendly and light introduction to our product was confusing and again, too techincal to understand.

The other big problem was with layout, specifically on the home page. Things that looked good on the web version didn’t look as good on mobile screens, an issue that I should’ve caught through user testing on mobile screens. Based off of this I went back and did just that, testing heavily on mobile versions of the site to guide my decisions on the web version.

Some issues on the home screen

More Testing

Based off of these tests I created a second version of the home page and conducted A/B tests. The first A/B test was simply on content. We wanted to see how digestible our copy was now compared to the initial version. The second A/B test was on our home page design. I asked qualitative questions such as “How did you feel about the sites?”, “Did anything interest you?”, and “Which version did you prefer between the two?”

Results

Our feedback from these tests were generally positive. The text was a lot more understandable, even to the average joe. However, it was not perfect. Some sections such as endorsements on the product page still confused readers, and the use cases on our home page seemed a bit too niche for our target audience (why isolate four industries when your platform is clearly targeting more?).

Users also preferred the second version of the homepage layout better than the first. Users felt that the site felt more together, and the content flowed from one section to the next, compared to the initial version which felt very blocky and isolated.

Design Decisions

Single Navigation

We wanted to use a flat navigation style for the website to keep things simple. We would primarily have ‘Home’, ‘About’, ‘Pricing’, and ‘Product’ as our core pages. On top of that, we wanted users to be able to jump around the architecture whenever it seemed appropriate so links were added in relevant sections.

Isometric Illustrations

I felt that isometric illustrations would be the most appropriate for our website. Not only does this touch on the blockchain aspect of our company, but the aesthetic was a solid fit with our brand as we wanted the sharpness to capture our efficiency and the block formation to promote building things together, step by step.

Alternating Preview

We really wanted to highlight the key features of Exalton which would present visitors with why we are better than our competitors. We chose to use a grid system that alternates left and right to accomplish this. Headers and subheaders are used to promote scanning.

Style Guide

I wanted to prominently display our dual color theme while having a minimal look. I wanted the site to be easy to navigate and attract visitors of all ages and industries.

Project Learnings

Steal Like an Artist

 Since I didn’t have all that much experience in graphic design, coming up with and creating our custom illustrations was a big mountain to climb. I had to use a lot of references on sites such as Dribbble, Pinterest, and even Google Images. Being able to have inspirations to build off of and make into my own helped a lot in the process and I definitely learned a ton about using images off of the internet as inspirations rather than a stencil.


Teamwork Makes the Dream Work

 Going through the design process validated my belief that teamwork is key, even if other team members aren’t in a design-centric major. Coming up with the visuals and layout for the site was a culmination of all of our opinions and as we kept having meetings and iterating through the design I was able to finalize something all of us enjoyed.



Next Steps

 Although our team is happy with the website design, I personally still think there is more room to grow. I wanted to veer away from the “Silicon Valley/Start Up” aesthetic with pastel colors and minimal artwork or photography. After browsing through dozens of websites for inspiration I noticed that a lot of them looked very similar to each other (pastel colors, minimal photographic direction, and those abstract humanoid figures). The ones that stood out to me are those with very well put together videos, 3D renderings, and interactive backgrounds. I hope in the coming months to grow my skills as a designer t o learn how to do more with new tools so I can add more unique elements to Exalton’s brand.
