Overview

Context

Exalton is a file management platform that is leveraging emerging technologies to bring in a new wave of collaboration. Since each organization tends to work isolated in their own world, we found that innovation begins bottlenecking at trust. Now, imagine a world where anyone, anywhere, can collaborate with each other without the risk of piracy or theft. This is the world Exalton means to create.

In the Spring of 2019 I had the pleasure of joining Exalton as their core designer. Although they had a solid foundation, and the research to back it up, I was tasked with turning their ideas into a reality. My priority was in their Desktop App, which was the main way we believed users would be interacting with this product.


Problem

Todays file managers fall short of being ideal for many businesses who heavily interact with their assets such as research labs or SaaS companies. The problem comes in three-fold: a lack of security of their files, an interface that considers how businesses internally organize their team, and a lack of automation for tasks that can be seen as ‘busy work’.


Solution

Exalton is built with a keen eye towards designing for organizations. Using a distributed ledger system to ensure that all files are within full control of the owner both online and offline has opened the floodgates on collaboration, especially between organizations. Your IP is guaranteed no matter who has access to your files. An interface inspired by popular file managers and Slack makes working in teams much more streamlined. The days of a cluttered and unorganized mass of files are no longer.

Design Process

Empathize

Interviews

To begin our research I first met with Erik (CEO) and Abinesh (CTO) to go over what findings they had made with initial customer interviews that gauged interest. We decided that further research was necessary so I began scheduling another round of interviews. I conducted 7 interviews total, 3 were with individuals in the biotech industry and the other 4 were officers in campus organizations such as TSE and EECS.


Interview Goals

  • Understand their experiences with file managers at work to gauge what is working and what is not.
  • Find what user’s core values are for their file manager and how those can apply to us.
  • Determine what ‘ideal solutions’ people believe in for whichever problems they have with their current file managers.

Key Findings

Based off our results, many people who use file-management platforms such as Dropbox or Google Drive expressed similar issues such as clunky workflows or poor organization of the site itself. We also found that certain labs on campus have built their own file managers since current ones on the market leave a lot to be desired still.

Across the board we saw that many companies and organizations refused to collaborate with outside entities due to security concerns.


Users value a clean and easily organized workspace.

A big complaint was just how disorganized some file managers can be. This is especially the case for power users who see numerous files come and go on a daily basis.

"We use our own file manager, but over the course of a project things get pretty messy. We've looked at various options but creating our own folders and sub folders is the only thing that works for us."

 X2, Lab Researcher at UCSD.

Collaboration only occurs if there is security.

We also saw that most organizations actually want to collaborate, but there simply isn't a reliable platform to do this with. Most PM's would not feel comfortable sending valuable files to another organization without a guarantee that those files would remain as their own IP.

"We've thought about partnering up with other firms, but there really is nothing stopping them from just stealing our product—unless we go through the trouble of filling out the paperwork. But honestly that would be way too annoying and slow."

X8, PM for LunaDNA

Keep it simple... stupid.

Users don’t want to get bogged down by all the technical aspects of the platform, especially not blockchain. Make it transparent what problem(s) are being solved without going to deep on how they are being solved. As long as we stay true to our promises, the UX will also stay, dare I say it, delightful.

“From my previous experiences with other blockchain companies, even the word ‘blockchain’ becomes more of a deterrent than it does a buzz word.”

X3, Researcher for BlockLab

Competitive Analysis

We continued our research by doing an analysis on companies who we felt would be a direct competitor (Box, Storj, Caplinked, and Biovia). We also took some time to analyze the top three file managers in the market today: Google Drive, Dropbox, and OneCloud.

Key Findings

Don't reinvent the wheel!

By now almost everyone has interacted with a file manager at one point in their life so there isn’t a need for us to make any dramatic changes (at least on a UI level). Follow Jakob’s law!


Keep the user in control and make sure they know it.

Users like being in control, especially if they’re working with files that have a lot of value behind them. The missing piece in so many of these file managers is that the amount of control they have is limited, especially when sharing a file.

Design Thinking

Since Exalton had only seven members, it was important that everyone was involved in understanding and defining the problem. Although they had a general idea of what the product should be, I thought it'd be best to lead the team through a few design tasks to solidify our objective and better relate with our user. We created an empathy map on a who we believed would use file managers frequently, specifically for work. We then went into Invision Freehand to collaborate on two journey maps.

Need Statements

From these exercises and the research we had done, we crafted four core pain points that our platform would intend to tackle. They essentially became the pillars that Exalton lives by.

Personas

We narrowed down our target users from “anyone who manages files” which was far too vague into specific roles within an organization. I created simple personas for each to consolidate the information for the team and myself.

The Problem

Across all industries, collaboration and organization are crucial for a strong product. However, a lack of trust and a failure for programs to mirror real-world projects and teams create a bottleneck. This leads to a less collaborative environment and clunky workflow.

The Solution

File Security (Collaboration)

Exalton’s end-to-end file sharing platform ensures that no matter who you send a file to, they are only able to do what you allow them to do. File traceability, immutability, and smart contracts strengthens the trust by automating it so cross-organization collaboration is encouraged.


Project Oriented Design

Exalton takes heavy inspiration from Slack’s workspace and channels layout to make it easier for organizations to locate and share files between projects or teams.


Automation

Cumbersome processes such as smart contracts and NDA’s are all automated to lessen busy work so organizations can focus on the work that matters.

Ideate

Design Thinking #2

Once we explicitly defined our problems it was time to review features that had already been discussed as well as consider new features that are congruent with our personas and need statements.

Big Idea Vignettes

Prioritization Grid

Key Features

From this we highlighted key features that would lead to our product’s success. Our priority still lied in creating the most secure file-sharing platform that enhances workflow through automation and UI organization. This section was especially important because we had to discuss what was feasible for our team since many of us are still undergraduate students.

Workspace & Channels

Reorganizing our file manager to better reflect how real organizations work together was crucial for the app. Being able to create teams (such as departments) allows files to be easily retrieved.

In light of our dream to bring in cross-organization collaboration, an overarching ‘Project’ workspace would increase organization and efficiency so users don’t have a mess of files all just in one place.

Access Hierarchy

In order to fully utilize our DLT, access to files and functions are limited based off of your roles. Admins are at the top, followed by team leads or company leads. This way, file accessibility can be automated from the get go.

Online & Offline Control

Lying behind the scenes is Exalton’s ability to control files whether you’re online or offline. This was crucial for security, especially if a user intends to collaborate with an outside organization. Instead of having to go through legal processes to ensure file security, users will find that if, for example, the owner doesn’t want the file to be downloaded, then that function will be locked off.

File Endorsements

We noticed that with some organizations, if a file were to be lost or pirated, they would see a huge financial loss. In order to enforce a stricter level of security, certain files will require endorsements by (usually) admins so that higher ups can have a greater oversight on what is happening to their files.

This was an especially tricky area to design for because the idea of endorsements and approvals are common in the blockchain industry, but for a typical user this could be confusing and frustrating. Further user testing will allow me to narrow down the design to account for this.

Auditable File History

Again, we wanted users to know exactly what was happening with their files. Similar to Google Drive, we provide a history of every action taken on the file so if someone deletes a file or shares it with someone, they can be identified. Admins will also have the ability to revert files to account for mistakes.

Workflow Automation

Powered heavily by smart contracts, we wanted to build a permissions system that would deal with the legal nitty gritty of file sharing. Users are able to increase efficiency by circumventing the need to get legal approval since this system will be built into the platform.

User Flows

I created two simplified task flows to help visualize navigation within the app. The first is mainly navigation of the app whereas the second is dedicated to sharing or requesting files.

Prototype &
Testing

Wireframes

During this stage, I was working closely with each member of the team. I consulted Erik (CEO) and Abinesh (CTO) to confirm that the app looked and felt how they wanted to while also communicating with the development team to ensure that each element was feasible to create.

User Testing

Once everyone was onboard with wireframes I started scheduling tests. In order to get the most useful data we knew that we couldn't only test our app on everyday users (such as students), but rather people who have worked in a professional setting with things like a project lead, teams, and sensitive files. Luckily, Vidit worked on an on campus lab which perfectly fit our conditions.

One core issue I found was with our information scent. Users had trouble accomplishing certain tasks because of this such as creating a new project or joining a team.

I decided to do a deeper analysis on the mainstream file managers such as Google Drive and One Drive. Since they were so common, having a similar layout to them may make navigating our own application easier. I did the same for Slack and Discord since we were implementing a channel/workspace layout.

Changing layout to match better with Google Drive. Clicking on a file should highlight and bring in a side menu. Right clicking with now display dropdown instead of left click.

More intuitive labels were also added, namely on our ‘Approvals’ page since this was one of the most differentiating aspects of our app compared to other file managers. ‘Approvals’ and ‘To Approve’ were altered to ‘All Files’ and ‘Pending’ respectively.

Hi-Fi Prototype &
More Testing

Afterwards, I converted the wireframes into a hi-fi prototype. I ran a heuristic evaluation with Nielsen's Usability Heuristics to see if there were any basic mistakes that slipped through. Once I felt satisfied, I scheduled another round of user testing with the same five lab researchers as well as two new ones. I felt that having some fresh eyes would resolve some testing bias.

Although the results were much better this time, I still ran into a few issues.

The first issue was in 'Teams'. I decided to run A/B tests on having the teams fill up a larger space on the side nav, versus a shorter panel. Some users did not know that this area was scrollable, and ended up not being able to find the specific team they were looking for.

With added visual design, users were much more able to understand that the area was scrollable. Version A actually turned up new problems as having sections of the nav completely disappear brought on a lot more confusion. In the end I decided to go with B, but with a gradient at the bottom to further show users that more lies below.

I ran another set of A/B tests for our ‘Projects’ panel. Both Slack and Discord (who use workspaces) have all workspaces always present, and multiple users did not know that projects could even expand into a new panel in our wireframe tests.

Based off of the results I had, version A was more preferred. Although other popular apps have a layout similar to B, the lab researchers I tested with brought up the point that a file manager will probably have a lot going on already, so dedicating so much real estate to navigation could get annoying. On top of that, we saw that files between projects would rarely cross paths and users who are involved with multiple projects tend to remain in one project workspace on a single day.

The last test I ran was on our ‘Approvals’ page. I chose to run a card-sorting activity with my participants to see in which order the labels should be presented in. As a result we switched the design over from ‘All Files’, ‘Pending’, ‘Admins’ into ‘Admins’, ‘Pending’ and ‘All Files’.

Maintain control over files

Exalton is capable of logging and enforcing activities regardless of whether files are developed inside or outside the application. This allows you to closely monitor and actively enforce any user’s permissions by leveraging embedded solutions into each user’s work environment.

SHARING PARAMETERS

Share your files however you want.

When sharing your file with others, you can specify an assortment of sharing parameters. By leveraging smart contracts, these sharing parameters can automatically execute to authorize, manage, and terminate a teammate’s access to files.

Exalton’s ledger enforces a permissioned consensus. Our notification and reporting system notifies network administers to authorize updates and to train the ledger orderers. Additionally, because the ledger system expands to offline monitoring, assets can safely be transacted across disparate development and continue to maintain an immutable record.

Project Learnings

Don't Reinvent The Wheel

I think as an industry, designers want to explore ways of making their design stand out above the rest, but what needs to be at the core of any design choice is the ‘why’. I think with this project it taught me a lot about keeping things simple. With apps such as Google Drive that are used by so many people, it’s better to have a recognizable design. Whatever ‘X factor’ that makes your app special should shine behind the scenes. There’s no need to reinvent the wheel.


One Step at a Time

Exalton is definitely the most expansive project I have ever taken on, especially being the only designer on the team. At first the vast number of components that had to be designed was daunting, but I learned that breaking components down to smaller parts and taking them on one step at a time greatly reduced my stress and improved my productivity.


Prioritize, Prioritize, Prioritize!

On top of taking things one step at a time, making sure that you have a plan as to what to work on first is crucial. Having deadlines I set for myself on certain pages or components was crucial in delivering designs on top. This was especially useful because I would face last minute requests for other assets such as a pitch deck or business card which could’ve derailed my whole timeline otherwise.



Reflection and Next Steps

 I was really grateful to have this opportunity to join Exalton and begin building their product, and within those three months I definitely felt like I learned a lot.
Given that this version of the app is only meant for the POC, there is still a lot more to design, to test, and to iterate on. These designs are nowhere near perfect, but Rome was never built in a day :). I'm excited to see what more I can learn and how I can apply my skills as a growing designer onto this project.
